MY OLYMPIC DIARY

by Dexter T. Higgins
FUTURE GOLD MEDALIST (PROBABLY) — PROPERTY OF DEX, DO NOT READ, SERIOUSLY MOM
MEDAL PROGRESS:
MONDAY — the day it all began —

Found a flyer stapled to the community board today. It said "OLYMPIC TRYOUTS, ALL AGES WELCOME." I did not read the small print. I never read the small print. Small print is for people who aren't destined for greatness.

I told my family at dinner that I am going to the Olympics. My little sister Pip laughed so hard she snorted milk. My dad asked which sport. I said "whichever one has the shiniest medal," which I think is a very strategic answer.

Started my Official Training Log. Wrote "DAY 1" on the cover in three different colors so it would look more legit. Have not actually trained yet. That's tomorrow's problem.

TUESDAY — training montage, sort of —

Attempted my first official training session. Tried to do push-ups. Got to one and a half. The half doesn't count but I wrote down two anyway, because Olympians round up.

Then I tried sprinting across the backyard to build speed. Tripped over Pip's scooter and did what my dad generously called "an unscheduled dismount." Grass stains: seven. Dignity: zero.

Attempted swimming training in the inflatable kiddie pool. It is approximately four feet long. I still managed to get a mouthful of water and a lecture from Mom about "using the pool responsibly."

Training Log, Day 2:
push-ups: 2 (ish)
sprints: 1 (crash-landed)
laps swum: 0.5
morale: sky-high

WEDNESDAY — the household strikes back —

Woke up ready to train and discovered Pip had "borrowed" my Official Training Log to draw unicorns in it. There is now a unicorn wearing my future gold medal. I have decided this is actually a good omen.

Baked a medal-shaped cookie out of gingerbread to use as motivation — look at the medal, want the medal, become worthy of the medal. The dog ate the medal before I finished the sentence. The dog is now, unofficially, the most decorated athlete in this house.

Mom made me do chores instead of training, "because the Olympics can wait, the recycling cannot." Reader, I took out the recycling like it was the shot put. Personal best: one bin, no dropped bottles.

THURSDAY — a hidden talent, discovered —

Went to the grocery store with Mom and, purely by accident, pushed the cart around a corner so smoothly that a stranger said "nice line." I have never felt so seen.

This is it. This is my sport. Competitive Shopping Cart Slalom. I spent the next twenty minutes weaving between the cereal aisle and the freezer section, setting what I am calling unofficial world records. Mom called it "Tuesday."

Practiced my victory wave in the cereal aisle mirror. It needs work. Practiced my "humble champion" face. Also needs work. Progress is progress.

Training Log, Day 4:
carts slalomed: 6
cereal boxes knocked down: 3
new sport discovered: 1

FRIDAY — the big day —

Arrived at Town Sports Day in my finest sweatband, ready for the Olympics. Turns out — and I am only finding this out now, at the check-in table — it is not, in fact, the actual Olympics. It is the county mini-games, run by Mrs. Ferris from the library, with events like the egg-and-spoon race and the three-legged sprint.

I will admit there was a moment of quiet devastation. It lasted approximately four seconds. Then I remembered I had trained my whole life (five days) for this exact moment, and small print or no small print, I was going to give it everything.

I did not win the egg-and-spoon race. I did not win the three-legged sprint (turns out you need a partner who runs the same direction as you; sorry, Pip). But I did win a paper medal for "Most Enthusiastic Athlete," presented by Mrs. Ferris herself, and let me tell you — it felt exactly as heavy as gold.

Updated my Official Training Log for the final time: Mission status: technically not the Olympics, emotionally the Olympics. Already planning next year's training regimen. Step one: find out what sport the shopping cart thing actually counts as.
